Chapter In·I

Elam

Sumer's Eastern Shadow
Every civilization in this Chronicle's Mesopotamian cluster has a scene in which Elam appears at the gate — sacking Ur, hauling off Hammurabi's law code, finishing the Kassites, allying with a dying Babylon against Assyria. This is the chapter that tells the story from the other side of the Zagros: a highland-and-lowland federation that outlasted Sumer, outlasted Akkad, and came within one Assyrian king of being erased from memory altogether — only to hand the Persian Empire that followed it half of its royal vocabulary. Prologue

I Sowed Salt

It is c. 646 BCE, and Assyrian soldiers are climbing down into the tombs of dead kings. Susa has fallen — the last act in a run of catastrophes that began, seven years earlier, with the severed head of an Elamite king put on display in a palace garden at Nineveh, Assyria's capital far to the north. Now the city itself is being taken apart. Temple statues of gods worshipped in this land for two thousand years are dragged into the light and carted away. Royal tombs — some centuries old, some barely closed — are pried open, and the bones inside are gathered up to be hauled back to Nineveh as trophies of humiliation, denying the dead kings of Elam even the dignity of rest. In the fields outside the city, by the Assyrian king's own later account, his men spread salt across the ground.

Dramatized. The siege and fall of Susa (dated c. 646 BCE, with some sources giving 647 or 645 BCE), the plundering of its temples, the desecration of royal tombs and removal of royal bones, and the devastation of Elam's agricultural land are recorded in Ashurbanipal's own inscriptions, one of which states in the king's own boast, "I devastated the provinces of Elam and on their lands I sowed salt" attested[1][7]; the display of King Teumman's severed head seven years earlier, in 653 BCE, is separately attested and detailed later in this chapter[12]. Modern archaeology confirms severe destruction at Susa in this period, though some historians now judge Ashurbanipal's claim of total annihilation to be royal exaggeration rather than literal fact inferred/disputed[3]. The soldiers' individual actions are invented framing around attested events.

That is how this story usually gets told — as an ending, a footnote to Assyria's long list of conquests. But Susa had already stood for well over three thousand years by the time Ashurbanipal's men climbed down into its tombs, and the kingdom built around it had, at one point or another, sacked the world's oldest city, humbled Sargon of Akkad's own dynasty, been addressed as "father" by Hammurabi of Babylon, hauled home the most famous law code ever carved in stone, and finished off the Kassite kings this Chronicle has already met. Assyria did not defeat a footnote. It defeated one of the oldest continuously significant states in the ancient Near East — and, in doing so, unknowingly cleared the ground for that state's own institutions to be inherited by the empire that would eclipse Assyria in turn.

Who and Where

Between Two Worlds

Elam occupied a stretch of southwestern Iran that modern geography still remembers by name: the provinces of Khuzestan and Ilam, both derived from "Elam" itself.[1] The Akkadian and Sumerian name for the land — elamtu, NIM — is usually read as "the high land" or "the highlands," a Mesopotamian lowlander's-eye view of a place whose most striking feature, from Babylon or Ur, was the wall of the Zagros mountains rising behind it. The Elamites themselves used a different name, Haltamti, whose meaning is less certain.[2]

That outsider's name hints at something structurally true about Elam: it was never one thing. Its heartland straddled two very different worlds — the hot, flat, river-fed lowlands of Susiana around the city of Susa, close enough to Mesopotamia to be pulled constantly into its politics, and the cooler highland basin of Anshan, in what is now Iran's Fars province, oriented toward the Iranian plateau and the peoples beyond it. For most of its history Elam was less a nation than a federation binding these two zones together, ruled by kings who styled themselves "king of Anshan and Susa" and who spent real effort holding highland and lowland loyalties in the same hand.[2] Susa itself, founded around the turn of the fifth to fourth millennium BCE, is one of the oldest continuously important urban sites anywhere on earth, inhabited on and off for roughly six thousand years.[11]

Elam also gave the ancient world one of its stubbornest mysteries, and it starts almost as early as the story itself. Around 3100–2900 BCE, at exactly the moment Mesopotamian proto-cuneiform was being invented next door, Susa developed its own independent accounting script for tracking goods and labor in a centralized economy — now called proto-Elamite. Roughly 1,600 tablets survive, the vast majority from Susa itself, with related finds as far east as Tepe Yahya near modern Kerman.[4] Scholars can read its numbers and its pictures of goods; the great majority of its abstract signs remain unread, and no one has ever confirmed what spoken language, if any, the script was even recording.[4] More than five thousand years on, proto-Elamite remains one of the last major undeciphered writing systems from the Bronze Age world — a mystery this chapter will return to, half-solved, at its very end.

The First Kingdoms

The Old Kings

Elam's political history opens in the shadow of its Mesopotamian neighbors, as it would so often afterward. The Awan dynasty (c. 2400–2100 BCE, with roots reaching earlier) ruled a kingdom already important enough for Mesopotamian kings to boast about beating it. Sargon of Akkad, founder of history's first empire, defeated the Awan king Luh-ishan and imposed Akkadian as Susa's language of record — a small, telling episode in the much larger story of Sargon's conquests already told in this Chronicle's Akkad chapter.Akkad & Old Babylon (I·2)[1]

The balance of power did not stay Mesopotamia's for long. Under the Shimashki dynasty that followed, Elam absorbed pressure from Ur's Third Dynasty — King Shulgi of Ur briefly held Susa itself — and then turned the tables completely. In 2004 BCE, an Elamite army under King Kindattu, joined by Susa's own restless population, marched on the greatest city in the world and broke it. Ur's king, Ibbi-Sin, was carried off into Elamite captivity and never returned; the Sumerians mourned their fallen capital in the great lament this Chronicle's Sumer chapter has already told in full.Sumer (I·1)[1] For a highland-and-lowland federation that Mesopotamian scribes routinely dismissed as barbarian raiders, it was a spectacular reversal: the oldest civilization on earth had just been ended, in part, by the neighbor it had spent centuries looking down on.

The Federation

Father of Kings

Elam's next dynasty, the Sukkalmah or Epartid line, built something genuinely unusual: a formal power-sharing federation at the top of the state. Supreme authority sat with a king called the sukkalmah, but beneath him ruled two named viceroys with their own titles — a "sukkal of Elam and of Simashki" governing the eastern highlands, and a "sukkal of Susa" governing the western lowlands — with succession often passing sideways to brothers before it passed down to sons, and royal legitimacy in some generations reinforced by marriages within the ruling family itself.[2] It was a system built, in other words, to keep Elam's two halves from pulling apart — a permanent answer to the same highland/lowland tension this chapter's opening section already named.

For roughly two centuries it worked spectacularly well. Under the sukkalmah Siwe-Palar-Khuppak, Elam became the dominant power of the entire Mesopotamian world — not through conquest but through sheer diplomatic weight. Letters recovered from the archive of Mari, on the Euphrates, show Hammurabi of Babylon and Zimri-Lim of Mari alike addressing him with the honorific "father," a term of formal deference no equal used lightly, and Elamite messengers reaching as far as Qatna and Emar in Syria.[1] It could not last. In the 1760s BCE, the same Hammurabi who had once called Siwe-Palar-Khuppak's successors "father" turned on Elam after it tried to play Babylon and its rivals against one another, driving Elamite influence out of Mesopotamia as the opening move in the campaign that built Hammurabi's own short-lived empire — a story this Chronicle's Akkad chapter has already told from Babylon's side.Akkad & Old Babylon (I·2)[1]

The Golden Age

Gods in One Place

The Kidinuid and Igihalkid dynasties that followed Elam's diplomatic peak pursued something quieter than conquest: a slow cultural project, spreading highland Elamite language, religion, and identity down into lowland Susa, a process modern scholars call "Elamization."[1] It reached its most ambitious expression around 1250 BCE, when King Untash-Napirisha founded an entirely new sacred city on empty ground roughly forty kilometers from Susa. He named it Dur-Untash — "the town of Untash" — and built at its heart a temple tower unlike anything else known from the ancient world: a ziggurat some 105 meters on each side, its five stacked levels rising in mudbrick faced with baked, inscribed brick, ringed by three concentric walls enclosing palaces, eleven further temples, and royal tombs sunk beneath the ground.[5]

Its purpose was explicit and unusual: to gather the gods of Elam's highlands and lowlands into a single religious capital, uniting a federation that had always been pulled two ways into one shared sacred center. Untash-Napirisha dedicated a stele there to Inshushinak, Susa's own patron god and judge of the dead, framing his kingship and the god's protection as a reciprocal bargain rather than one-sided royal boasting — an unusually explicit piece of political theology for the period.[5] The project outlived its king without ever being finished: when Untash-Napirisha died around 1240 BCE, construction simply stopped, leaving mudbricks stacked and ready beside temples that were never completed. By the archaeologists' own reading of the evidence, the great temple at the heart of the complex may never have been put to the religious use it was built for at all — though the wider sacred city went on drawing pilgrims for roughly two more centuries before it, too, was finally left empty around 1000 BCE.[5][13]

Attested: Untash-Napirisha's construction of Chogha Zanbil/Dur-Untash c. 1250 BCE, its dimensions, dedication to Inshushinak, incomplete construction, and its use as a pilgrimage site until roughly 1000 BCE are documented archaeologically and epigraphically[5][13]. Framing language around "political theology" is this Chronicle's interpretive gloss on attested facts, not a direct ancient claim.

Dur-Untash then lay forgotten under a low mound — locally known as Chogha Zanbil, "basket hill" — for roughly six hundred years longer, rediscovered only in 1935 when a geologist prospecting for oil noticed an inscribed brick protruding from the earth. Formal excavation followed under Roland de Mecquenem and then, far more extensively, under Roman Ghirshman between 1951 and 1962. In 1979 it became Iran's first UNESCO World Heritage Site, honored today as the best-preserved stepped temple tower anywhere in the world — the unfinished centerpiece of a highland king's dream now the single most complete monument Elam left behind.[5]

The Conquerors

Trophies from Babylon

Elam's third and most aggressive Middle Elamite dynasty, the Shutrukids, converted centuries of highland-lowland state-building into raw military power. Shutruk-Nakhkhunte and his three sons broke the Kassite dynasty that had ruled Babylon in relative peace for nearly four centuries, killing the Babylonian king Zababa-shuma-iddin around 1155 BCE (some sources date the decisive campaign slightly earlier, to 1158 BCE) and ending Kassite rule outright — the same collapse this Chronicle's account of Babylon under the Kassites has already described from the losing side.Babylon: Kassites to the Fall (I·3)[1] As his armies withdrew, Shutruk-Nakhkhunte hauled home an extraordinary haul of Babylonian trophies: a statue of the god Marduk himself, the Obelisk of Manishtushu, the Victory Stele of Naram-Sin, and — most famous of all — the black stone stele bearing the Code of Hammurabi, carried off from Babylon and re-erected in Susa as a monument to Elamite triumph, its own long afterlife as war booty and museum object already traced in this Chronicle's Babylon and Akkad chapters.Babylon (C·I)[1]

Susa's own art from this era, rather than Babylon's stolen monuments, best shows what Elamite craftsmanship could do on its own terms. In 1903 the French excavator Jacques de Morgan uncovered, in the temple of the goddess Ninhursag at Susa, a life-size royal statue unlike anything else to survive from the ancient Near East: the Statue of Napir-Asu, queen and wife of Untash-Napirisha, cast not in stone but in metal — a clay-and-brick core wrapped in carved wax, encased in a further clay mold, then fired so the wax melted away, leaving a void into which molten copper was poured to form the outer shell; once that had cooled, the interior clay was dug out again and further layers of bronze were poured inside to build up a solid, freestanding figure.[6] The finished statue weighs roughly 1,750 kilograms — the largest known example of Elamite bronze sculpture — and its fringed skirt carries a curse in Elamite cuneiform, invoking the gods Napirisha, Kiririsha, and Inshushinak against anyone who defaces it, ending with the wish that the offender's own name "become extinct."[6] No looter ever tried; the statue survived intact for over three thousand years and stands today in the Louvre, a monument not to conquest but to a metalworking skill Mesopotamia itself never matched at this scale.

The Missing Centuries

The Quiet Centuries

Elam's Shutrukid triumph did not last. Babylon found its own champion in Nebuchadnezzar I, who defeated the last Shutrukid king, Khutelutush-In-Shushinak, and drove Elamite power back out of Mesopotamia for good. What followed was nearly three centuries in which Elam almost vanishes from the historical record — no major inscriptions, no dynastic king-lists, only silence, the mirror image of the equally quiet centuries this Chronicle's account of Kassite Babylon has already described on the Mesopotamian side of the same collapse.Babylon: Kassites to the Fall (I·3)[2] Whatever happened inside Elam during these centuries — whether internal fragmentation, highland-lowland drift, or simply a documentary dark age rather than a real one — the sources are not there to say.

The Last Alliance

Return to the Stage

Elam re-enters clear history in the late eighth century BCE as the Neo-Assyrian Empire's most persistent headache in the east. King Humban-nikash I backed the Babylonian rebel Merodach-baladan against Sargon II of Assyria; his successor Shutruk-Nahhunte II, the last Elamite king to claim the old title "king of Anshan and Susa," was routed by Assyrian forces in 710 BCE.[1] The pattern repeated for a generation: Elam and a rebellious Babylon leaning on each other against Assyrian power, occasionally seizing Babylon itself — as Hallutash-Inshushinak briefly did in 694 BCE — and meeting Assyria's armies head-on at the bloody, inconclusive Battle of Halule in 691 BCE, which a joint Elamite-Babylonian force fought against Sennacherib to a draw that both sides' own inscriptions claimed as victory.[1] Sennacherib's subsequent destruction of Babylon in 689 BCE, and Babylon's own long afterlife in this Chronicle's Babylon chapter, followed directly from this alliance's failure to hold.Babylon (C·I)

The alliance bought Elam a generation of relevance, but it also meant Elam had chosen the losing side of Assyria's imperial century — and it made an enemy that would not forget.

The Ending

Salt in the Ground

The final collapse opened with a usurpation. Around 653 BCE an Elamite noble named Teumman seized the Elamite throne and demanded that Ashurbanipal hand over the sons of the previous king, who had fled to Nineveh for shelter; when Ashurbanipal refused, Teumman allied with rebels in Babylon and marched to war.[12] At the Battle of Ulai (also called Til-Tuba), fought on the banks of a river near Susa, the Assyrian army crushed Teumman's forces outright; Teumman himself was killed on the field along with his son, and his severed head was carried back to Nineveh, where Assyrian palace reliefs later showed it hanging in a garden as Ashurbanipal and his queen dined beneath it — one of the most shocking images to survive from the ancient Near East, and one the Assyrians themselves commissioned to advertise, not conceal.[12]

Elam never recovered its footing after Til-Tuba. Ashurbanipal spent the following years installing and discarding a series of rival Elamite claimants as he fought a separate, grinding civil war against his own brother, the Assyrian-appointed king of Babylon — and when that war ended, Ashurbanipal turned his full attention back east. His armies campaigned into Elam repeatedly through the late 650s and 640s BCE, and around 646 BCE they took Susa itself — the siege and sack this chapter opened with, temples plundered, royal tombs broken open, and the land, in the king's own boast, sown with salt.[1][7] The last Elamite king to rule in his own right, Humban-Haltash III, was hunted down and captured around 640 BCE, ending close to two thousand years of Elamite kingship, on and off, since the Awan dynasty first appears in the record.[1]

It was not, in the end, quite the total annihilation Ashurbanipal's scribes described. Excavation shows real, severe destruction at Susa in this period, but also continued occupation afterward — a kingdom broken as an independent power, not erased from the map inferred/disputed.[3] Weak, shadow rulers still claimed the old Elamite royal titles for a few more decades. But the federation that had once made Hammurabi call its king "father" was never again an independent state. Within roughly a century, Elam's own former highland heartland — Anshan — would give its name and, in large part, its institutions to a very different power: Persia.

The Bridge

What Elam Became

Assyria broke Elam as a state, but it did not erase what Elam had built. Within a few decades, the Medes and then the Persians — highland neighbors who had spent centuries in Elam's cultural orbit — filled the vacuum Ashurbanipal's armies had left behind, and when Cyrus the Great forged the Achaemenid Persian Empire in the sixth century BCE, he did not present himself as a conqueror sweeping Elam aside. He called himself, among his other titles, "king of Anshan" — claiming, quite deliberately, Elam's own old highland capital and royal vocabulary as the foundation of his own legitimacy, a detail this Chronicle's Achaemenid Persia chapter has already introduced from the Persian side.Achaemenid Persia (II·4)[1]

The debt ran deeper than a royal title. Elamite became one of three official languages of the Achaemenid administration, alongside Old Persian and Babylonian — the language this Chronicle's Persia chapter has already followed onto the trilingual Behistun Inscription that let modern scholars crack cuneiform in the first place.Achaemenid Persia (II·4) But Elamite's deepest Achaemenid afterlife sits not on any monument but in an archive: the Persepolis Fortification Tablets, tens of thousands of clay documents excavated from two rooms in Persepolis's fortification wall by Ernst Herzfeld in 1933–34, recording the day-to-day rations, travel permits, and administrative bookkeeping of the Persian court almost entirely in Elamite — the empire that conquered Elam's homeland ran its own daily paperwork in Elam's own language, for generations.[10] Spoken Elamite outlived even that: medieval Arabic sources describe a language called "Khuzi" still spoken in Khuzestan as late as the tenth century CE, which some scholars connect to a final, very late survival of Elamite itself — a language living on, unofficially, some sixteen centuries after its last independent kingdom fell.[1]

And the oldest mystery this chapter opened with is not entirely a mystery anymore. Proto-Elamite, the script invented alongside Mesopotamian writing itself, remains almost entirely unread. But a related, later script called Linear Elamite — used at Susa roughly 2300 to 1850 BCE, known from just 51 surviving texts — was the subject, in 2022, of a "near-complete decipherment" claimed by the French archaeologist François Desset and colleagues, who identified royal names and the god Napirisha embedded in repeating sign-sequences on a set of inscribed silver vessels and argued the script recorded sound rather than whole words, which would make it one of the oldest purely phonetic writing systems ever devised.[8][9] Other specialists remain unconvinced, and full translations are still being debated inferred/disputed — a four-thousand-year-old federation still, in a small way, arguing with the world that conquered it about exactly what it was trying to say.[8]

Elam never got its own empire chapter in the history most people learn. It got absorbed — into Persian titles, Persian administration, Persian religious geography, and finally into the footnotes of the empires that came after Persia. But the land whose name survives today in two Iranian provinces broke the world's oldest city, humbled an Akkadian emperor's dynasty, was called "father" by the man who wrote history's most famous law code, and quietly handed the Achaemenid Empire its highland capital, part of its bureaucracy, and a language its own scribes kept using for well over a century after Elam itself had stopped existing as a state. That inheritance is the story this Chronicle has already told once, from Persia's side. This chapter has told it from the side of the kingdom that gave it away.
